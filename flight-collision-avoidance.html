Flight collision avoidance

Every year, around 40 aircraft are involved in a mid-air collision. Unfortunately, half of these are fatal. Surprisingly, most of these accidents happen in good visibility and daylight. Accident investigations have shown that the VFR-principle of “see and avoid” is not sufficient. In this increasingly crowded airspace paraglider pilots cannot afford to ignore this problem because we're the most vulnerables.

More than once you may have noted how difficult it is to find your flight buddie when you lose the sight of him during a cross country flight. The same applies to a sailplane, a helicopter, a drone or a hanglider: they can't see you. We should not say anything of airplanes because your are supposed to avoid the air space reseved for them. Although sometimes, ocasionally the problem can come from they don't respecting your airspace. Anyway, even if you could see them, the limited maneuverability of a paraglider would make it very dificult not only avoid a collision but to assure enough clearance to avoid the turbulence created by the other aircraft. We should find a way to become more visible to the rest of airships that share our airspace.

Primary surveillance radar, ADS-B, ELS, EHS transponders are surveillance systems mandatory for IFR heavy aircrafts. FLARM is the emerging technology that attempts to fill the regulatory void for light aircraft. Over 40,000 manned aircraft and many UAVs are already equipped with FLARM and the number is rapidly increasing. FLARM systems are available from several manufacturers for powered airplanes, helicopters, gliders, and UAVs.

FLARM works by calculating and broadcasting its own future flight path to nearby aircraft. At the same time, it receives the future flight path from surrounding aircraft. An intelligent motion prediction algorithm calculates a collision risk for each aircraft based on an integrated risk model. When a collision is imminent, the pilots are alerted with the relative position of the intruder, enabling them to avoid a collision. 

The obstacle database upgrades the FLARM system to also warn about imminent obstacle collisions with fixed objects such as cable railways, power lines or antenna masts stored in the database.

There are several FLARM certified flight instruments suitable for hang gliding and paragliding and several more are on the horizon. Oudie from Naviter and XC Tracer have prices ranging from 400€ to 800€ depending on its features. Their RF receivers can cover distances of up to 30 km.

But here we can talk about a low cost but high quality module: Primer Edition MKII is a DIY module based on the TTGO T-Beam board with LoRa chip which is available for direct purchase at a price of 25€. The firmware SoftRF has been developed as an open source project by Linar Yusupov on github.

LoRa is a wireless data communication technology that uses a radio modulation technique that can be generated by Semtech LoRa transceiver chips. It can communicate and receive different popular protocols including FLARM, OGNTP, FANET and the Skytraxx protocoll. FLARM and OGNTP seem to be very “wide known” in smaller aircrafts, such as gliders and motorplanes, balloons etc. The TTGO T-Beam board has also bluetooth and wifi to connect to your favourite flight app XCSoar, LK8000, FlySkyHy through FLARM NMEA sentences.
